History of Hip Hop & RNB

This page is for the real ones — the ones who grew up on bass lines, mixtapes, and lyrical heat. Hip hop isn’t just music, it’s how we speak, dress, move, and survive. From a Bronx basement to global stages — this is our culture, and this is our tribute to it.

Legendary Artists

  • Tupac Shakur — More than an artist, a voice for the unheard.
  • The Notorious B.I.G. — Smooth flow, raw stories, Brooklyn royalty.
  • Lauryn Hill — Soul, truth, and bars that cut deep.
  • Kendrick Lamar — The modern prophet of our generation.

Regional Influence

  • East Coast: This is where it all began — gritty beats, complex bars. You feel the hunger in every line from Nas, Biggie, Jay-Z and more.
  • West Coast: A sound born in the sun and soaked in funk. N.W.A, Snoop, Tupac — their stories came from the streets and hit the world hard.
  • South: The South brought the bounce. From Outkast to Lil Wayne to Future, southern rap has always had its own flavor, and now it dominates the charts.
  • Midwest: Fast flows, real talk. Think Kanye’s creativity, Twista’s speed, and Bone Thugs’ harmony. The Midwest is the underdog with heart.

Major Moments in Hip Hop

1973: Birth of Hip Hop

DJ Kool Herc throws a back-to-school party at 1520 Sedgwick Ave in the Bronx — looping the breakbeats live on two turntables. That party planted the first seed of hip hop culture.

1982: Hip Hop Hits TV

Hip hop hits mainstream eyes when Afrika Bambaataa performs “Planet Rock” and shows how futuristic and funky rap can be.

1986: Run-DMC & Aerosmith

The collaboration on “Walk This Way” smashes boundaries — merging rock and rap and bringing hip hop into suburban homes.

1992–1997: East vs West

Tupac and Biggie symbolize the intensity of East Coast vs West Coast rivalries — lyrical battles turn real, and hip hop loses two legends too soon.

2000s: Southern Takeover

From Outkast to Lil Jon to T.I., the South flips the sound. Crunk, trap, and bounce redefine hip hop’s energy. Atlanta becomes the new capital of rap.

2010: Drake Era Begins

Drake drops "Thank Me Later" and reshapes hip hop with emotional honesty, melodic rapping, and huge cultural influence. The streaming era takes off.

2012–2015: Sampling Revival

New-gen artists like Joey Bada$$, J. Cole, and Kendrick Lamar start flipping old soul samples and golden era drums, mixing the past with the future.

2020: Hip Hop in the Pandemic

With the world locked down, artists like Megan Thee Stallion, Pop Smoke, and Lil Baby dominate TikTok and keep the game alive without live shows.

2023–Now: Global + AI Era

Hip hop becomes a global language — with artists worldwide making waves. At the same time, AI remixes and deepfake tracks start challenging what “real” means in the art.
